Xeno & Arbor — Instructions

For WS 10.2: Classification  ·  CORE103 Spring 2026

Part 1: Ague

1.1 Getting started

  1. In the top-left dropdown, make sure ague is selected.
  2. Set the mode selector on the left to training.
  3. Click make new cases to generate 10 training cases.

The Xeno control panel — malady selector top-left, mode selector on the left, make new cases button in the center.

What you're looking at: Each row is one alien creature. The Xhealth column is what you want to predict — sick or well. Everything else (hair color, eye color, etc.) is a potential feature.

1.2 Making a graph

  1. In the CODAP toolbar, click the graph icon to open a new graph.
  2. Drag a column header from the data table onto the x-axis.
  3. Drag Xhealth onto the y-axis.
  4. Look at how the dots separate — does one value of your feature line up mostly with sick? Mostly with well? Or is it a mess?
  5. Try different features until you find one that gives a clear separation.

1.4 Building a tree in Arbor

  1. Click the tree button in the upper-right corner of Xeno to open Arbor.
  2. Drag Xhealth from the data table into the tree panel — this is the thing you want to predict, and it goes in first.

    The root node appears, showing the overall split of sick vs. well in your training data.

  3. Drag the feature you identified into the tree. It creates a split with two branches.

    Two branches appear — one for each value of your feature. The leaf nodes are empty until you assign diagnoses.

  4. Click each leaf node to assign a diagnosis: sick or well.

    A finished tree — each branch ends with a diagnosis. Ready to test.

1.5 Testing your tree

  1. Change the mode selector from training to auto.
  2. Click run tree. Xeno generates 10 new cases and applies your rules automatically.
  3. Count how many it got right. That's your accuracy.
Why new cases? The real test is whether your rule works on creatures you've never seen before — that's what auto mode checks.

Part 2: Botulosis

Switch the malady dropdown to botulosis, then click make new cases to get 10 training cases.

Use the same steps as Part 1: make graphs to explore the data 2.1, write your rule in plain English before touching Arbor 2.2, build the tree 2.3, then switch to auto and run it 2.4.

Hint: Botulosis may require more than one feature. If one feature doesn't give you perfect accuracy, try dragging a second feature onto one of the leaf nodes to add another level to the tree.

Part 3: Dengueso

Switch to dengueso and get 10 training cases. The key feature here is a number, not a category — so you'll need to set a cut point in Arbor.

3.3 Using a numeric feature (the gear icon)

  1. Drag your numeric feature into the tree as usual. Then hover over the feature name in the tree — a gear icon appears to its left.

    Hover over the feature name to reveal the gear icon.

  2. Click the gear icon. Set your cut point and choose the operator (greater than or less than).

    Set the number where you want to draw the line, and which side predicts sick.

  3. Close the panel and check that the branches look right, then test in auto mode.
Choosing a cut point: Look at your graph from Exercise 3.1. Where does the boundary between sick and well seem to fall? Pick a number in that gap.

Part 4: Sumonoma — The Competition

Switch to sumonoma and get 20 training cases. Build your tree the same way as before. You can get more cases at any time — but the competition scores you on new cases, not the ones you trained on.

4.3 Sharing your CODAP document

  1. In CODAP, click the hamburger menu (☰) in the top-left corner.
  2. Select Share > Get link to shared view.
  3. Copy the link and paste it into Exercise 4.3 on your worksheet.
What this does: Creates a read-only snapshot of your CODAP document — data and tree — that your instructor can use to evaluate your model on 100 new cases.
